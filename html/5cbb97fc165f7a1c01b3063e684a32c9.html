Feeling rejected? Misunderstood? Where’s God? Welcome to the first Christmas — Terry Pluto

  • Updated: Dec. 23, 2023, 4:53 a.m.|
  • Published: Dec. 23, 2023, 4:50 a.m.
Subscribers can gift articles to anyone
By
  • Terry Pluto, cleveland.com

CLEVELAND, Ohio — Imagine if on the first Christmas, Mary and Joseph were like many of us.

The holidays come around, and some of us want everything to be … perfect. We want people to act right, or at least how we think they should behave. Some of us like to feel like we’re in control of most things in our life, and that’s tested this time of year. Schedules change. Routines are shattered. Strange relatives show up, old family messes bubble up.

Welcome to Christmas.

I thought about this when preparing my Christmas message to be given to the men at Akron’s Haven of Rest City Mission.

SHE IS WHAT???

Joseph and Mary were a traditional young Jewish couple. Their families came together to form an agreement for the young couple to be married. He was a carpenter. She was excited about being married to a guy with a decent family and a steady job.

Then God showed up. So did problems.

That happens sometimes when God shows up and pulls us in a different direction – one where we have very little control of what comes next.

Because Mary became pregnant, there was pressure to call off the wedding. That was a big scandal in those times, because an engagement was a business deal between two families and announced in front of people of the village. It was a major community event.

There seemed to be very little special about Mary, except in God’s eyes. She was called to bring the Son of God into the world, as is recorded in Luke 1. It was a miraculous pregnancy.

The family and friends of Joseph probably told him to dump her. Under the law at the time, she even could be stoned. At the very least, she would be sent away from the village to have the baby elsewhere.

Instead, Joseph listened to a dream where an angel told him to stick with Mary, that she indeed was told the truth about her pregnancy.

What does this have to do with us? I know many people who find themselves in the same situation as Joseph, called upon to raise a child who is not their own biologically. Often, it happens suddenly, just as it happened in the Bible.

TIME TO PAY TAXES

As Mary and Joseph worked through the pregnancy situation and decided to continue to be married, the Roman rulers called for a “census.” This actually was a demand for everyone go to the town of their family origin to register so they could pay taxes.

Mary was now very pregnant. They lived in a town called Nazareth. Joseph had to go to Bethlehem, the city of King David and his relatives.

It was 70 miles … walking … through the heat and rocks and snakes and dust and whatever else you find in the desert.

There are paintings of a pristine Mary riding serenely on a donkey led by Joseph. Maybe she was on a donkey, but that is not mentioned anywhere in the Bible. As for her moods, she was about ready to give birth.

Remember, it’s 70 miles on foot over several days. Not exactly a Holly, Jolly Christmas in the making.

Both of them had to wonder, “Where is God in all this? This trip to sign up for taxes … I mean, TAXES? We’re talking about TAXES???”

NO ROOM AT THE INN

When they arrived at Bethlehem, the small town was packed with visitors there to register. Joseph knew his wife was close to delivering the baby.

Imagine how their moods were at the end of the trip, and they discover “There was no room for them in the inn (Luke 2:7).”

Ever been in a situation of caring for someone and it seems like no matter what you do … IT JUST KEEPS GETTING WORSE!

I studied the culture of the time and the place where Jesus was born and grew up. The circumstances and rumors about Mary’s pregnancy may have led to there being “no room at the inn.” There would have been a lot of Joseph’s relatives in town for the census. Some also may have lived there.

In Mary’s hometown of Nazareth was where a small group of Roman soldiers were stationed. Local women sometimes fell in with them. Other times, they were sexually assaulted. That could have been the source of some of the rumors about Mary.

It also could be why Mary and Joseph were rejected when looking for a place to stay.

All together now, everyone say: “Family Mess.”

Isn’t it true that Family Mess often becomes an epidemic this time of year?

BORN IN A BARN?

Nowhere in the Bible does it say Jesus was born in a barn. It does talk about Jesus being “wrapped in swaddling clothes and laid in a manger.”

A manger is a trough used to feed animals. Some scholars believe Jesus was born in a cave, not a barn. That’s where some animals were housed at that time.

Here’s what I think: WHO CARES?

Barn? Cave? It ain’t the Ritz Carlton or even the Motel 6. No one kept the light on for them. If there’s a manger, there probably were animals around.

At this point, Mary and Joseph must have been thinking something like, “When will all this end? I thought God was in this.”

When we are taking care of someone old or young … someone in need … and it seems like no one else cares, don’t we stare at the heavens (at least for a moment) pleading for help?”

THE WELCOMING COMMITTEE

On the hills outside of Bethlehem were shepherds, doing what shepherds still do – watching their sheep.

Luke 2: 9-12: “An angel of the Lord appeared to them, and the glory of the Lord shone around them, and they were terrified. But the angel said to them, ‘Do not be afraid. I bring you good news that will cause great joy for all the people. Today in the town of David a Savior has been born to you; he is the Messiah, the Lord. This will be a sign to you: You will find a baby wrapped in clothes and lying in a manger.’ "

The shepherds mostly lived outside. They were not allowed in the main part of some temples because of their appearance and reputation.

Yet, God called them first to see Jesus. It’s symbolic of how Jesus came for everyone – born into a working-class family and welcomed by the shepherds, who knew about rejection in their own lives.

But Mary and Joseph had to look up and say, “God sent us shepherds?”

They probably would have preferred a visit from a wealthy relative.

But shepherds would have been comfortable in the barn/cave setting with animals. They would have been willing to help and not look down on the young couple.

Those of us who have been pressed into caring for someone know there are times when aid comes from the most unlikely sources.

Jesus was born in Bethlehem to fulfill the Old Testament prophecy of Micah 5:2: “But you, Bethlehem Ephrathah, though you are small among the clans of Judah, out of you will come for me one who will be ruler over Israel, whose origins are from of old, from ancient times.”

THE BIG POINT

Scholars and others will probably disagree with my account of the first Christmas. They love to fight over details and historical references.

But the heart of the story is more powerful than the often romantic versions found in media and books.

Jesus was born into a Jewish area occupied by the Roman army. His parents had to deal with rejection, rumors and at least some feelings that no one understood.

To bring Jesus into the world, they had to sacrifice a lot. Their journey was one that would have driven anyone with the slightest degree of being a control freak absolutely crazy.

They had to walk in faith and somehow trust God was in the middle of their suffering and frustrations.

One of my favorite lines is an old African-American Church saying: “When God shows up, it’s rarely early.”

That was very true of the first Christmas.
