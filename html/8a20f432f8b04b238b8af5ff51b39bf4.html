Life and Death Before the War: Israel, Gaza and the West Bank by the Numbers

The vital statistics of Israel and the Palestinian territories show deep disparities in a region now further ravaged by war.

By Christopher Wolf and Julia Haines
|
Nov. 14, 2023
|
By Christopher Wolf and Julia Haines
|
Nov. 14, 2023, at 1:08 p.m.

Who Lives in Israel and Gaza?

Life in Israel and the Palestinian territories has been upended since an Oct. 7 surprise attack on Israel by the terrorist group Hamas, which killed more than 1,000 people and sparked a counteroffensive by the Israeli military in Gaza that has killed thousands more.

A Timeline of the Israel-Palestinian Conflict

The region that today is home to both Israelis and Palestinians has been rife with conflict along ethnic and other lines for decades, and events throughout its complicated history have contributed to disparate qualities of life. Data points help tell the story of the people who live in Israel, Gaza and the West Bank, and what their life was like before the current war.

Age

The population density of Gaza rivals that of America’s largest cities, with more than 2 million people living on a roughly 140-square-mile strip of land. About 40% of Gaza’s population is 14 years old or younger and the territory’s median age was just 18 in 2020, making Gaza home to one of the world’s 20 youngest populations, based on figures from the CIA’s World Factbook.

The West Bank’s median age was also low, at 21.9 years, ranking it among the 50 youngest populations based on World Factbook data. Relatively few people in the Palestinian territories are in their senior years, as estimates indicate people at least 65 years old make up less than 3% of Gaza’s population and about 4% of the West Bank’s population. Large shares of the population in both Gaza and the West Bank are Palestinian refugees.

In Israel, an estimated 12.4% of the population is 65 or older, and the country’s median age of 30.4 years was roughly on par with the global median age of 31.

Life Expectancy

Israel ranks among the top 10 countries worldwide for life expectancy, with an estimated average life expectancy of 83.5 years.

Meanwhile, people born in Gaza today can expect to live for 75.7 years, and in the West Bank, 76.6 years. While both territories' estimates are lower than Israel’s, life expectancy in each is projected to be longer than the world’s mark of 70.5 years.

Religion and Ethnicity

The vast majority of people in the Gaza Strip and the West Bank are Muslims who predominantly practice Sunni Islam, though hundreds of thousands of Jewish Israelis have settled in the West Bank. And while Jewish people make up the majority of the population in Israel (74%), Muslims account for about 1 in 5 people there (18%).

The stats are similar for ethnic groups: Palestinian Arabs are the dominant group in both Gaza and the West Bank, while about 21% of the Israeli population is Arab. The 120-member Knesset – Israel’s unicameral legislative body – featured its lowest number of Arab representatives (10) in two decades when newly elected members were sworn in a year ago.

War in Israel and Gaza

Poverty and Unemployment

Since Hamas took control of Gaza in 2007, Israel – supported by Egypt – has blockaded the territory and tightly controls the movement of people and goods in and out.

An International Monetary Fund report says more than 60% of Gazans recently lived in poverty, while the West Bank’s share was 19%. Gaza’s unemployment rate was 44% at the end of 2022, according to the report.

Many of the best jobs available to Gazans are located across the border in Israel and require highly coveted work permits issued by the Israeli government. Israel detained at least some permitted workers and deported thousands in the wake of the Oct. 7 Hamas attack.

The West Bank’s unemployment rate was 12% at 2022’s end. The combined unemployment rate for the two Palestinian territories was estimated to be 25.7% in 2022, among the top five worst unemployment rates for countries and territories tracked by the World Bank that year.

The unemployment rate in Israel that year was much lower at 3.5%, similar to that of the U.S.

Gross Domestic Product

Both Palestinian territories have struggled economically for decades. The territories’ gross domestic product per capita, adjusted for purchasing power and inflation, hasn’t exceeded $6,500 in the West Bank and Gaza in World Bank figures that date back to 1994.

In 2022, the territories’ combined GDP per capita was about $5,700, nearly eight times lower than Israel’s, at about $44,300, and less than 10% of the United States’ figure of approximately $64,700.

Gaza has received billions of dollars in foreign aid in recent years in efforts to alleviate burdens posed by the blockade. The U.S. has given several hundred million dollars to the Palestinian territories since 2021 – a ramp-up following drastic cuts to aid in later years of the Trump administration.

However, the U.S. sends far more aid to Israel, which has received more U.S. foreign aid than any other country since World War II, including more than $3 billion in fiscal 2021 alone.

Health and Education

In 2022, data indicates 30% of the population of the West Bank and Gaza combined were not using safely managed sanitation services and 1 in 5 were not using safely managed drinking water services, though these shares were lower than global estimates.

In Israel, 96% of the population was using safely managed sanitation services and 99% used safely managed drinking water services – with the latter rate higher than that of the U.S. After the current war began, Israel shut off supplies of water into the Gaza Strip. The country reportedly restored water access in southern Gaza, though supplies have remained limited.

Infant and maternal mortality rates were estimated to be approximately four to six times higher in Gaza and the West Bank than in Israel as of 2020, though the combined territories performed better than the world overall. Maternal mortality in the United States was slightly worse than in the Palestinian territories, according to 2020 estimates provided by the World Bank that were determined by statistical models.

For many people in Israel and the Palestinian territories, core values emphasize education. Twice as many primary school-aged children were not in school in the West Bank and Gaza compared with Israel in 2021. For children that did attend, Palestinian teachers likely had classrooms with twice as many pupils as Israeli teachers.
